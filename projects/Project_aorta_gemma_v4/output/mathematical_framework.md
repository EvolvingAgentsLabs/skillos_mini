# Mathematical Framework: Quantum Homomorphic Cepstral Analysis for Arterial Navigation\n\n## 1. Signal Model Definition\n\nIn the context of Project Aorta, we model the hemodynamic pressure signal $s(t)$ captured by a catheter-tip sensor as a composite signal consisting of the primary cardiac excitation and its subsequent reflections from arterial structures (bifurcations, stenoses, or vessel walls).\n\nWe define the discrete-time signal model as:\n$$s(t) = p(t) + \\alpha \\cdot p(t - \\tau) + \\epsilon(t)$$\n\nWhere:\n- $p(t)$: The primary cardiac-driven pressure pulse (the source excitation).\n- $\\alpha$: The reflection coefficient, determined by the impedance mismatch $\\Gamma = \\frac{Z_2 - Z_1}{Z_2 + Z_1}$.\n- $\\tau$: The time delay (quefrency) representing the propagation time of the echo relative to the source.\n- $\epsilon(t)$: Stochastic physiological noise and sensor white noise.\n\nIn a more general sense, the signal is a convolution of the excitation source $S(t)$ and the arterial impulse response $H(t)$:\n$$s(t) = (s_{source} * h_{artery})(t) + \\epsilon(t)$$\n\n## 2. Fourier Analysis Derivation\n\nTo transition to the frequency domain, we apply the Continuous Fourier Transform (CFT) or the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) for computational implementation. Applying the linearity and time-shifting properties of the Fourier Transform to the signal model:\n\n$$\\mathcal{F}\\{s(t)\\} = S(\\omega) = \\mathcal{F}\\{p(t) + \\alpha \\cdot p(t - \\tau)\\}$$\n\nUsing the shift theorem $\\mathcal{F}\\{p(t - \\tau)\\} = e^{-i\\omega\\tau} P(\\omega)$:\n\n$$S(\\omega) = P(\\omega) + \\alpha e^{-i\\omega\\tau} P(\\omega)$$\n$$\n$$S(\\omega) = P(\\omega) (1 + \\alpha e^{-i\\omega\\tau})$$\n\nThis representation demonstrates that the spectrum of the recorded signal is the spectrum of the source modulated by a complex periodic function (the transfer function of the reflection).\n\n## 3. Homomorphic Decomposition Proof\n\nStandard Fourier analysis fails to separate $P(\\omega)$ from the reflection component because they are multiplicative in the frequency domain. Homomorphic decomposition utilizes the logarithmic property to transform multiplication into addition.\n\nLet $A(\\omega) = |S(\\omega)|$. Taking the natural logarithm:\n$$\\ln|S(\\omega)| = \\ln|P(\\omega) (1 + \\alpha e^{-i\\omega\\tau})|$$\n$$\n$$\\ln|S(\\omega)| = \\ln|P(\\omega)| + \\ln|1 + \\alpha e^{-i\\omega\\tau}|$$\n\nLet $L(\\omega) = \\ln|S(\\omega)|$. We define two components:\n1. **Spectral Envelope**: $E(\\omega) = \\ln|P(\\omega)|$, which represents the slow-varying physiological characteristics of the cardiac pulse.\n2. **Homomorphic Component**: $R(\\omega) = \\ln|1 + \\alpha e^{-i\\omega\\tau}|$, which contains the periodic structure related to the reflection delay $\\tau$.\n\nBy separating these components, we can isolate the structural information from the excitation signal.\n\n## 4. Cepstral Domain Interpretation\n\nThe Cepstrum $c(n)$ is defined as the Inverse Fourier Transform (IFFT) of the log-spectrum:\n\n$$c(n) = \\mathcal{F}^{-1}\\{\\ln|S(\\omega)|\\}$$\n\nSubstituting our decomposition:\n$$c(n) = \\mathcal{F}^{-1}\\{\\ln|P(\\omega)|\\} + \\mathcal{F}^{-1}\\{\\ln|1 + \\alpha e^{-i\\omega\\tau}|\\}$$\n\nIn the cepstral domain (quefrency domain):\n- The term $\\mathcal{F}^{-1}\\{\\ln|P(\\omega)|\\}$ contributes to the low-quefrency components (the \"lifter\" or spectral envelope).\n- The term $\\mathcal{F}^{-1}\\{\\ln|1 + \\alpha e^{-i\\omega\\tau}|\\}$ manifests as a sharp peak (impulse) at the specific quefrency $n = \\tau$.\n\n**Peak Detection**: The navigation coordinate is derived from the location of the maximum amplitude in the cepstral domain: $\\hat{\\tau} = \\arg\\max_n |c(n)|$.\n\n## 5. Quantum Operator Mapping\n\nTo achieve real-time processing in high-dimensional dynamic environments, we map the classical cepstral pipeline to quantum operators.\n\n| Classical Operation | Quantum Operator | Mathematical Mapping |\n| :--- | :--- | :--- |\n| Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) | Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT) | $|j\\rangle \\to \\frac{1}{\\sqrt{N}} \\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} e^{2\\pi i jk/N} |k\\rangle$ |\n| Magnitude Extraction | Amplitude Estimation / Measurement | $P(\\omega) \\to \\||\\psi\\rangle\\|^2$ |\n| Logarithmic Operator | Quantum Logarithm / Variational Unitary | $\\hat{U}_{log} |x\\rangle = |\\log(x)\\rangle$ |\n| Inverse DFT | Inverse QFT (IQFT) | $|k\\rangle \\to \\frac{1}{\\sqrt{N}} \\sum_{j=0}^{N-1} e^{-2\\pi i jk/N} |j\\rangle$ |\n\n## 6. Circuit Complexity Analysis\n\n### Classical Complexity\nThe FFT for $N$ samples scales as $O(N \\log N)$. In high-frequency hemodynamic monitoring with large $N$, this becomes a bottleneck for real-time feedback loops.\n\n### Quantum Complexity\nThe QFT scales as $O(\\log^2 N)$ (or $O(\\log N)$ with certain approximations). This exponential speedup allows for the processing of extremely high-resolution pressure data with negligible latency.\n\n### Requirements\n- **Qubits**: $n = \\lceil \\log_2 N \\rceil$ qubits for the signal representation, plus auxiliary qubits for logarithmic arithmetic.\n- **Gate Sequence**: \n  1. State Preparation (Amplitude Encoding of $s(t)$).\n  2. QFT circuit.\n  3. Unitary approximation of $\\ln(\\cdot)$ (using Taylor expansion or variational quantum circuits).\n  4. IQFT circuit.\n  5. Measurement in the computational basis to extract quefrency peaks.\n\n## 7. Parameter Specifications for Implementation\n\n- **Sampling Frequency ($f_s$)**: $\\ge 2$ kHz (to capture high-frequency harmonics of the pressure pulse).\n- **Quantization Depth**: $16$-bit minimum for pressure sensor data to maintain log-domain precision.\n- **Quefrency Resolution**: $\\Delta\\tau = 1/f_s$, aiming for sub-millimeter spatial resolution equivalent to $\\Delta\\tau \\cdot c_{pulse}$ (where $c_{pulse}$ is the pulse wave velocity).\n- **Target Error Tolerance**: $\\sigma_{\\tau} < 0.1$ ms.\n
