# Whitepaper: Quantum Cepstral Deconvolution for Arterial Pressure Wave Echo Separation\n\n**Project Code Name:** Echo-Q  \n**Phase:** 4 (Synthesis & Final Documentation)  \n**Status:** Final Technical Specification  \n\n---\n\n## Abstract\n\nThis whitepaper details the design and theoretical framework of the Echo-Q algorithm, a quantum-enhanced approach to cepstral deconvolution. The primary objective is the separation of echo components from arterial pressure waves, a critical task in hemodynamic monitoring. By leveraging the exponential speedup of the Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT) and the universality of the Quantum Singular Value Transformation (QSVT), Echo-Q provides a scalable pathway for high-resolution signal deconvolution on near-term and fault-tolerant quantum architectures.\n\n## 1. Theoretical Foundation\n\nThe Echo-Q algorithm is built upon three core pillars of quantum information theory:\n\n### 1.1 Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT)\nAs defined in [[concepts/quantum-fourier-transform]], the QFT provides the mechanism to shift signal information from the time domain to the frequency domain with $O(n^2)$ complexity. This is the fundamental step in calculating the power spectrum required for cepstral analysis.\n\n### 1.2 Quantum Singular Value Transformation (QSVT)\nTo address the non-unitary nature of the logarithmic operation required for cepstral computation, we employ QSVT [[concepts/quantum-singular-value-transformation]]. QSVT allows us to apply a polynomial approximation $P(x) \approx \ln(x)$ to the singular values of a block-encoded operator, effectively performing the \"logarithm\" step within a unitary framework.\n\n### 1.3 Block-Encoding\nNon-unitary operators are embedded into unitary matrices via block-encoding [[concepts/block-encoding]]. This allows the deconvolution operator to be applied to the quantum state while maintaining the unitarity required by the laws of quantum mechanics.\n\n## 2. Algorithm Design\n\nThe Echo-Q algorithm follows a structured quantum circuit pipeline to compute the cepstrum $\mathcal{C}(n) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}(\ln(\mathcal{F}(s(t))))$.\n\n### 2.1 Step-by-Step Workflow\n1.  **Amplitude Encoding**: The classical signal $s(t)$ is mapped to a quantum state $|\psi\rangle = \sum s_i |i\rangle$ ensuring $\sum |s_i|^2 = 1$ [[concepts/block-encoding]].\n2.  **Frequency Mapping**: The QFT is applied to transform the state into the frequency domain $|S\rangle$.\n3.  **Logarithmic Approximation**: The QSVT block is executed using a Chebyshev polynomial sequence to approximate the logarithm of the amplitudes.\n4.  **Cepstral Mapping**: The inverse QFT ($\text{QFT}^{\dagger}$) is applied to return the state to the cepstral domain.\n5.  **Peak Detection**: Measurement reveals the cepstral coefficients, where the echo delay $\tau$ manifests as a distinctive peak.\n\n### 2.2 Circuit Architecture (ASCII Representation)\n\n```text\n|0> --- [ State Prep ] --- [ QFT ] --- [ QSVT (Log) ] --- [ IQFT ] --- [ Measure ]\n|1> --- [ Amplitude ] --- [      ] --- [ Polynomial ] --- [      ] ---\n|...                                                                  |\n|n> ------------------------------------------------------------------\n\nDetail of QSVT Block:\n\n|Aux> --- [ Phase Shift φ₁ ] ---\n|Reg> --- [ Block-Encoded A ] ---\n|Aux> --- [ Phase Shift φ₂ ] ---\n```\n\n## 3. Constraint Verification Table\n\nTo ensure algorithmic validity, all implementations were measured against the invariants defined in [[concepts/sentient-state-constraints]].\n\n| ID  | Constraint Name | Status | Verification Method |\n|:---|:---|:---|:---|\n| C1.1 | Unitarity | **PASS** | All operators (QFT/QSVT) are unitary by design. |\n| C1.2 | Norm Preservation | **PASS** | Input normalization $1/\|s\|$ applied during encoding. |\n| C2.1 | Polynomial Bounds | **PENDING**| Requires $\epsilon < 10^{-3}$ convergence testing. |\n| C2.2 | Block-Encoding Scale| **PASS** | Normalization factor $\\alpha$ tracked in amplitude scaling. |\n\n## 4. Implementation Notes\n\nThe current implementation, found in `output/quantum_cepstrum.py`, utilizes the Qiskit framework. Key design decisions include:\n- **Normalization**: Explicit normalization to prevent amplitude divergence.\n- **Complexity Management**: Using `qiskit.circuit.library.QFT` for optimized gate decomposition.\n- **Simulation**: Targeted for the `AerSimulator` to validate logic in a noise-free environment before hardware deployment.\n\n## 5. Results & Discussion\n\nPreliminary simulations suggest that the Echo-Q algorithm can successfully identify echo delays in synthetic sinusoidal signals. The primary challenge remains the degree of the Chebyshev polynomial required to maintain high precision in the logarithmic approximation without exceeding the coherence time of NISQ devices.\n\n## 6. Error Recovery Journal\n\n- **Issue**: Divergence in amplitudes during non-unitary operations.\n- **Resolution**: Implemented strict normalization and tracked the $\\alpha$ factor in the block-encoding step (see Constraint C1.2).\n- **Issue**: High gate depth in QFT.\n- **Resolution**: Evaluation of Approximate QFT (AQFT) is recommended for future NISQ-specific iterations.\n\n## 7.
